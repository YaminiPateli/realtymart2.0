Everything you need to know

The dynamics of the real estate market often draw parallels with the stock market, each exhibiting its ebb and flow, making sense in hindsight. Furthermore, both markets mirror the country's economic landscape, presenting enticing investment prospects. However, it's imperative to grasp the inherent risks and opportunities

Investing in Stocks:

The realm of stock investment boasts an inherent potential for robust profit margins, surpassing many other asset classes. Stock market investments come with the advantages of liquidity and flexibility, qualities that real estate investments often lack. Stocks also tend to offer growth rates that outpace the relatively slower-paced real estate market.

Investing in Real Estate:

Real estate investments often find their foundation in homeownership, representing one of the most fundamental forms of such investment. Diverging from stocks, real estate constitutes a tangible asset, providing a deeper sense of psychological comfort, security, and satisfaction. Additionally, the return on investment in real estate tends to exhibit a degree of consistency, primarily due to the phenomenon of property appreciation. In contrast, stock markets are renowned for their greater unpredictability.

In your younger years, consider investing as much as 300% of your total assets by utilizing loans for your initial home purchase. According to experts, it's advisable to ensure that your cumulative monthly mortgage payments do not surpass 30% to 35% of your gross monthly income. Many first-time homebuyers often get swept up in the excitement of acquiring a new home without fully considering the financial implications. Setting overly high expectations for your budget can lead to financial stress down the road. It's recommended that your Equated Monthly Installment (EMI) should not exceed 30-40% of your take-home salary.

In cases where the property market in your city is prohibitively expensive and your dream home seems out of reach, a prudent approach is to invest in a property that falls within your budget, even if it's on the outskirts of the city. Look for reputable developers offering properties that align with your financial capabilities. By entering the market early, during the launch stage, and strategically exiting, you can potentially benefit from property value appreciation, creating a foundation of seed capital for your future.

Additionally, many banks offer the flexibility to transition from one loan to another. This means you can sell a more affordably priced property in a peripheral location and utilize the proceeds as seed money to purchase your desired residence. Failing to adopt such financial strategies may leave you lagging behind the market in terms of your overall financial well-being.

Typically, whenever property ownership changes hands, there is a requirement to pay Stamp Duty. This duty is assessed based on the total value of the property being transferred. It's important to note that the specific amount of Stamp Duty can vary significantly from one city to another.

When determining your monthly home loan installment, it's crucial to take into account both your current monthly family income and the anticipated income in the future. Family income encompasses not only your earnings but also those of your parents or spouse, if applicable. Equally vital is a comprehensive assessment of your family's existing expenses, encompassing all other loans you are currently repaying. As a prudent guideline, it's advisable not to allocate more than 50% of your total income toward your monthly Equated Monthly Installment (EMI).

Typically, your primary bank, where you maintain your salary account and have established most of your banking relationships, is likely to offer you the most favorable interest rate. Additionally, many banks have preferred or invitation pricing schemes in place, allowing you to reap the advantages of these exclusive offers.

Keep in mind the importance of assessing the property that is being held as collateral for a bank loan. Initially, your responsibility will be to settle the bank loan, after which you can proceed with transferring the property into the buyer's name. It's also possible for you, the new buyer, and the bank to collectively execute the agreement concurrently.

Prior to making any investments, it's advisable to conduct thorough research online and conduct on-site visits to assess ongoing projects. Consider allocating your investments to projects that have reached a completion rate of at least 25-30% as this tends to expedite the approval process and provide a greater level of comfort. It's worth noting that in some instances, brokers may present more competitive rates compared to the developer's in-house sales team. Additionally, projects that have received bank approvals are often favored due to the added assurance they provide in terms of the approval process.

Attempting to forecast returns over a mere two-year span is a challenging endeavor. When considering property investment, it's crucial to adopt a long-term perspective. Although certain properties in metropolitan areas have demonstrated significant price increases ranging from 20% to 80% in the past two years, it's essential to recognize that this may not necessarily reflect future trends. Typically, more substantial returns are realized when investments are held for at least four years before considering an exit strategy.

Anticipating the appreciation of land value hinges on the parallel development of infrastructure in sync with the upscale residential projects underway in many urban centers. The progression of key factors such as water supply, electricity, safety measures, law enforcement, road networks, and other amenities is pivotal. In areas where these aspects are advancing, it's reasonable to expect an increase in land value.

Selecting the right location is a paramount consideration when investing in property. Often, individuals inquire about the ideal location to maximize their investment returns. The property's connectivity to the city and other key destinations is a pivotal factor in augmenting its resale value. Buyers should ensure that the property is situated in a prime area with convenient access to bus stops, train stations, airports, supermarkets, and other essential amenities.

Tax Implication

1. What taxes do you need to pay when buying a property?

If you're buying a brand-new property, there are a few taxes and charges to keep in mind:

  • Stamp Duty – A government tax paid for property registration.
  • GST (Goods & Services Tax) – Only applies to under-construction properties. No GST for ready-to-move-in homes.
  • Registration Charges – A small official registration fee.

For resale properties, GST doesn’t apply — only stamp duty and registration charges are payable.

2. When is the property sale considered final?
  • When you receive possession (keys and control).
  • Or when the Sale Deed is registered at the sub-registrar office.

Once you have the keys or the deed is registered, the property is yours.

3. When do you need to pay Capital Gains Tax — and how can you save on it?

Capital Gains Tax is due when you sell a property at a profit:

  • Long-term gain: Property held for more than 2 years.
  • Short-term gain: Property held for less than 2 years.

To save on tax:

  • Buy another property within 1 year before or 2 years after the sale.
  • Invest in Capital Gain Bonds under Section 54EC within 6 months of sale.

Tip: Consult a tax advisor for personalized advice.

Documents & Legal Formalities

1. What documents and formalities are needed when buying a property?
  • ID Proof: Voter ID, PAN, Aadhaar, Passport, or Driving License.
  • Address Proof: Ration Card, Utility Bill, or Passport.
  • PAN Card: Required for high-value transactions.
  • Passport-sized photos
  • Sale Deed / Agreement to Sell: Must include complete ownership chain if resale.

Pro Tip: Always verify original documents, not photocopies.

2. What to check before signing an agreement?
  • Flat/unit number, tower and floor details
  • Area breakdown – Carpet, built-up, super built-up
  • Cost breakup – Base price, GST, parking, club charges, etc.
  • Possession date and any delay penalty
  • Payment schedule tied to construction/bank loan

Tip: Read carefully or get legal advice before signing.

3. How to know if a project has legal approvals?
  • Bank Approval: Reputed bank loan = likely cleared project
  • Title Deed: Should be clear and marketable
  • Approved Plans: Construction must match approved blueprints
  • Location Check: Avoid low-lying or water-reclaimed land
  • Utility Clearances: NOCs from electricity, water, sewage boards
  • CC & OC: Commencement and Occupancy Certificates are must-haves
  • Registered Sale Agreement: Ensure proper stamping and registration
